Chapter 9 Lagrangian Multipliers for Constraint Optimisation

In this Chapter we will briefly consider the optimisation of continuous functions subjected to equality constraints (this will be covered extensively in the 3rd year course), that is the problem: \[\begin{equation} \mbox{minimize} \ \ z=f(\mathbf{x}) \end{equation}\] subject to: \[ g_i(\mathbf{x})=b_i \] where \(f\) and \(g_i\) are differentiable. The Lagrange function, \(L\), is defined by introducing one Lagrange multiplier \(\lambda_i\) for each constraint \(g_i(\mathbf{x})\) as: \[\begin{equation} L(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{\lambda})=f(\mathbf{x})+\lambda\sum_{i=1}\left[b_i-g_i(\mathbf{x})\right ]\tag{9.1} \end{equation}\] The necessary condition of optimality are given by: \[\begin{equation} \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial x_j}= \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_j} + \sum_{i=1}^m \lambda_i\dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_i} = 0, \ \, \dfrac{\partial L}{\partial \lambda_i}= g_i(\mathbf{x}) = 0\tag{9.2} \end{equation}\]

9.0.1 Example

Use Lagrangian multipliers to minimise: \[ f(x_1 , x_2) = x_1^2 + 4x_2^2 \] subject to: \[ x_1 + 2x_2 = 1 \]

Solution:

\[ \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} - \lambda \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_1} = 0, \] \[ \dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} - \lambda \dfrac{\partial g}{\partial x_2} = 0, \] and \[ g(x_1,x_2) = b. \] Therefore, we solve: \[ 2x_1 - \lambda =0, \] \[ 8x_2 - 2 \lambda =0, \] and \[ x_1 + 2x_2 = 1. \] Solving these three equations we obtain \(x_1 = \dfrac{1}{2}\), \(x_2 =\dfrac{1}{4}\) and \(\lambda = 1\). Therefore, the optimum is: \[ f(x_1 , x_2 ) = \frac{1}{2}. \]

9.0.2 Exercises

  1. A length of wire \(L\) metre long is to be divided into two pieces, one in a circular shape and the other into a square. What must be individual lengths be so that the total area is a minimum. Formulate the optimisation problem mathematically and then solve.
  2. Minimise \[f(\mathbf{x})={x_1}^2+{x_2}^2\] subject to \[x_1+2x_2+1=0\]
  3. Find the dimensions of a cylindrical tin of sheet metal to maximise its volume such that the total surface area is equal to \(A_0 = 24\pi\).
